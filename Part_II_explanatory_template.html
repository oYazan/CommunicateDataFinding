Investigation Overview

In this presentation, I'll share key insights from my analysis of Prosper's peer-to-peer lending platform data. The analysis focused on understanding what factors influence interest rates (BorrowerRate) and loan outcomes, providing valuable information for both potential borrowers and investors on the platform.

The exploration revealed several important patterns in how Prosper assigns interest rates and which factors are most strongly associated with successful loan repayments.

Dataset Overview and Executive Summary

The Prosper Loan dataset contains information on 113,937 loans with 81 variables each, including loan amount, borrower rate, loan status, borrower income, and many other features that characterize both the loans and the borrowers.

Key findings from the analysis:

  1. Risk-based pricing model: Prosper uses a sophisticated model where interest rates strongly correlate with the borrower's Prosper Rating, which effectively predicts loan outcomes.
  2. Loan term impact: Longer-term loans (60 months) consistently have higher interest rates than shorter-term loans (36 months), regardless of borrower creditworthiness.
  3. Loan size and rates: Larger loans tend to have somewhat lower interest rates, particularly for highly-rated borrowers.
  4. Debt-to-Income relationship: Higher debt-to-income ratios correlate with higher interest rates and higher default probabilities, indicating this is a key risk metric in Prosper's assessment model.
  5. Employment status importance: Employed borrowers receive more favorable interest rates compared to part-time or unemployed borrowers.
  6. Successful loan profile: Completed loans generally have lower interest rates, reasonable loan amounts relative to income, and lower debt-to-income ratios compared to defaulted loans.

Prosper Rating and Interest Rates: Risk-Based Pricing

This visualization shows how Prosper uses a risk-based pricing strategy. There is a clear relationship between the Prosper Rating assigned to borrowers and the interest rate at which they lend. Borrowers with higher ratings (AA, A) always receive much lower interest rates than those with lower ratings (E, HR). This rating system is the linchpin of Prosper's lending model and effectively stratifies borrowers into certain risk categories.

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 7))

plt.title('Interest Rates by Prosper Rating', fontweight='bold')

means = rating_data.groupby('ProsperRating (Alpha)')['BorrowerRate'].mean()
for i, rating in enumerate(['AA', 'A', 'B', 'C', 'D', 'E', 'HR']):
    if rating in means.index:

                 ha='center', fontweight='bold')

plt.tight_layout()

Interest Rates by Loan Term and Rating: The Time Effect

This visualization shows how Prosper Rating and loan duration affect interest rates. In each rating class, longer-duration loans (60 months) carry higher interest rates than shorter-duration loans (36 months). This shows that time horizon is a fundamental risk element in Prosper's model, regardless of the credit quality of the borrower. Even the most creditworthy borrowers (AA-rated) pay significantly more for longer-duration loans, reflecting the greater uncertainty of longer repayment periods.

term_rating_data = df.dropna(subset=['ProsperRating (Alpha)', 'Term'])

term_rating_data = term_rating_data[term_rating_data['Term'].isin([36, 60])]

grouped_data = term_rating_data.groupby(['ProsperRating (Alpha)', 'Term'])['BorrowerRate'].mean().reset_index()

pivot_data = grouped_data.pivot(index='ProsperRating (Alpha)',
                                columns='Term',

Loan Success Factors: A Multivariate Analysis

This visualization investigates the way various variables interact to determine loan outcomes. The pairplot reveals different patterns between completed/current loans and charged-off or defaulted loans.

Key takeaways:

  1. Charged-off and defaulted loans have much higher interest rates
  2. Default rates increase as debt-to-income ratios increase
  3. Successful loans have bigger loan sizes in relation to income
  4. Individual monthly income in and of itself is not an effective predictor of loan performance, yet its relationship to other variables does matter

This multi-factor view demonstrates loan success as predicated upon interaction among various rather than individual factors, which portrays the advanced analysis of credit risk.
